# Imara — Trademark Conflict Risk Brief

**Date:** 21 June 2026
**Prepared for:** Ruan (founder) + a SA trademark attorney
**Status:** factual risk-scoping research — **NOT legal advice.** A registered SA trademark attorney must give the formal clearance opinion before any go-to-market or rebrand decision.

---

## Bottom line: **HIGH risk.** Treat a pre-launch rebrand as the default; only deviate if a CIPC search + attorney opinion clears it.

The name "Imara" collides with an established, same-sector, same-region incumbent. The three factors that drive trademark conflict — **identical name + overlapping services + overlapping territory** — are all present, which is close to the worst-case pattern. The prudent, lowest-regret move is to decide the name question *now*, before brand equity accrues, because rebranding after launch is far costlier than before.

---

## 1. The conflict — who the incumbent is

**Imara Group / Imara Holdings** is an established African financial-services group (founded ~1990s; holding company structured 2002, Gaborone, Botswana), operating in **asset management, stockbroking, corporate finance, and fiduciary/administration** directly and through partners across ~10 African countries plus a London office, **with South African entities** (Imara Capital South Africa (Pty), Imara Asset Management South Africa (Pty); it previously owned Imara S.P. Reid, sold to MMI Holdings for ~R120m).

**The sharpest fact:** the group positions *itself* as **"Africa's leading non-bank financial services group"** that **"specializes in investing and advising in the African fintech and payment sectors."** So Imara isn't merely adjacent to Imara-the-tool — it is in **financial services and fintech, in Africa, under the identical name.** That is the core of the problem.

*Mitigating nuance (do not over-weight it):* "Imara" is a common Swahili word ("strong / firm / stable"), so it is somewhat less distinctive than a coined term and other entities use it. Weaker distinctiveness slightly thins protection in the abstract — but it does **not** cure a head-to-head conflict between two financial-services brands of the identical name in the same market. The incumbent's prior use in the exact sector is what matters.

## 2. The South African law

- Infringement test — **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, s34(1)(a):** unauthorised use of an identical mark, or one "so nearly resembling it as to be likely to deceive or cause confusion," for the goods/services it's registered for. Courts do a **holistic** assessment (visual, phonetic, conceptual similarity + relatedness of the services + the relevant consumers). **Identical mark + same/similar services = the strongest case for confusion.**
- **Registration is not required for the incumbent to act.** Even an *unregistered* mark is protected by the common-law tort of **passing off** (a misrepresentation that your business is, or is connected with, theirs). Imara Group's long prior use in SA financial services gives it passing-off standing regardless of its registration status.
- **Company-name registration ≠ trademark rights.** Registering "Imara …(Pty) Ltd" at CIPC gives no trademark protection, and a registered trademark holder can challenge a confusingly similar company name. So securing a CIPC *company* name buys no safety here.
- **The narrow escape hatch:** SA law allows continued use where the second user operates in a **genuinely different market sector with no likelihood of confusion.** That door is *barely open here* because both parties are in financial services / SME finance / fintech — the opposite of a different sector.

## 3. Classes at risk (Nice Classification; CIPC adopted the 13th edition on 1 Jan 2026)

SA uses a **single-class** filing system (one application + fee per class). The tool would plausibly need:
- **Class 9** — downloadable/SaaS software.
- **Class 36 — financial services / financial analysis / credit & financial information. THIS IS THE DANGER CLASS** — it is exactly where a financial-services incumbent would hold or claim rights.
- **Class 42** — SaaS / software-as-a-service, hosting.

A clash in **class 36** (and likely 9/42) is the realistic exposure.

## 4. Coexistence vs. rebrand

- **Coexistence agreements** (a private contract letting both use the name under agreed limits) work best when the parties are in **distinct geographies or distinct industries.** Here, geography (SA) and industry (financial services/fintech) both **overlap**, which is precisely when coexistence is hardest to obtain and weakest — and it requires the incumbent's *cooperation*, which a larger established group has little incentive to give a new entrant using its name.
- The consistent professional guidance: when the conflict is **strong (same/near-identical mark on the same/closely related services), the smart move is to start fresh with a new name** — and to do it **before** building brand equity, because a forced rebrand later (after a cease-and-desist or opposition) causes brand dilution, customer confusion, legal cost, and operational friction at the worst possible time.
- Rough economics: a coexistence agreement is ~$2k–$10k in legal fees *if obtainable*; contested trademark litigation runs into the hundreds of thousands. A pre-launch rebrand is the cheapest option of all.

## 5. CIPC process, cost, timeline (to clear/secure whatever name you land on)

- **Search:** free basic search on the CIPC IP portal; a **professional registrability/phonetic search via an attorney ≈ R2,500 + VAT** (catches confusingly-similar and phonetic hits the free search misses). **Do this first.**
- **File:** Form **TM1**, official fee **R590 per class** (so 3 classes ≈ R1,770 in CIPC fees); attorney professional fees typically **R3,000–R10,000+**.
- **Timeline:** examination ~**6–9 months** if unopposed, up to **9–18 months** with objections; published in the Patent Journal for a **3-month opposition window** (during which Imara Group could oppose).
- **Term:** 10 years, renewable indefinitely (TM5 renewal R260/class).

## 6. Recommended next actions (ordered, concrete)

1. **Run a CIPC trademark search on "Imara" in classes 9, 36, 42 — before anything else.** A free portal search today to see what's registered; then a **professional attorney search** for the definitive picture (does Imara Group / any party hold live SA registrations in these classes?). This single step resolves the one open unknown in this brief.
2. **Get a one-page attorney clearance opinion** on "Imara" for an SME-finance SaaS in SA, given the incumbent. Cheap relative to the downside.
3. **In parallel, shortlist 3–5 alternative names** and run quick CIPC + domain + common-law checks on them, so a rebrand (if advised) is ready rather than rushed. Decide the name **before** the design-partner pilot puts the brand in front of lenders/accountants — i.e. before equity accrues.
4. **If you keep "Imara":** only do so on a written attorney opinion, and consider seeking a **coexistence agreement** with Imara Group defining the SME-decision-support niche — but plan for the realistic chance they decline.
5. **Either way, register the chosen mark** (classes 9/36/42) to stop the *next* entrant and to strengthen your own position.
